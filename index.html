Proximity Risk with Covid-infected Places for Mobile Users:

A Big Data Machine Learning Approach

Guanyao Li, Tianlang He, Gary Chan

Department of Computer Science and Engineering

The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology

{gliaw, theaf, gchan}@cse.ust.hk



Covid-19, especially its Omicron variant, is spreading fast globally taking toll on many lives. The infection is often caused by exposure to the virus for sustained period of time due to close distancing with the contaminated places, the so-called proximity risk. Proximity risk is related to not only the exposure duration and physical distance to the Covid-infected places, but also one’s location noise and the number and nature of the venues (restaurants or places with large number of confirmed cases are often of higher risk). Based on the public data accessible from the CHP (Centre for Health Protection) of HKSAR government, this work is to provide timely information to mobile users of the infected places in their neighborhood, and assess in private the proximity risk with these places over time. Armed with such knowledge, users may then take appropriate actions to plan routes, manage one’s health, and keep safe distancing from these places.

To inform citizens, the CHP has already made available to the general public the places with infected cases (“incident places”) such as building with case resided/visited in the past 14 days, buildings requiring compulsory testing, and StayHomeSafe buildings (buildings with quarantined patients). However, these data are currently presented as a static webpage, and require users to check manually without real-time GPS support for mobile users. Some apps leverage the data by asking users to input locations in order to check if they are in proximity to the incident places. However, they are mostly not automatic, and offer no assessment of proximity risk over time for users to take appropriate proactive measures.
A heatmap of infected places in Hong Kong.
Based on our past research, we have designed and developed a novel, private and automated mobile-friendly app which integrates the updated the open data from CHP and visualizes them as an easily accessible heatmap pinpointing incident places as hotspots. Mobile users may browse the heatmap to understand where the places with infected cases are, and make informed decision on whether they should keep safe distancing from them. They may optionally turn on their GPS to check automatically and locally the proximity risk in real time. Our mobile computing system makes use of the following approaches:
  • Big data mining to assess proximity risk [1, 2]: We integrate the open and trustworthy data sources from CHP and present it as an informative, mobile-friendly and user-accessible heatmap for browsing, visualization and understanding at a quick glance where the incident places are. The heatmap is updated continuously according to government release. If the user turns on GPS, the heatmap can be used to automatically pinpoint one’s current location with respect to these areas, and alert the user if there are such buildings in proximity. The app also computes locally user proximity risk based on the distance, location noise, dwell time, number and nature of the places in the neighborhood. The number of places in proximity is charted in real time and the overall proximity risk is indicated as a color bar. Based on this information, the user may take timely and appropriate actions to manage health, plan routes and keep proper distancing to reduce infection risk:

    Red: Overall high sustained proximity level to incident places. Recommended to avoid high-risk places, manage health and take voluntary testing if necessary.

    Yellow: Medium risk. Be cautious. Plan safe paths to reduce risk.

    Green: Low risk. Stay vigilant.

  • Machine learning for power-conserving GPS sampling [3]:To conserve battery consumption due to GPS, we have devised and implemented a light-weight machine learning approach to adaptively adjust GPS sampling according to user mobility level. This can greatly reduce the GPS sampling rate, and hence power consumption, by more than 90%.

    User privacy is our primary design consideration. Our system is fully distributed without maintaining any server to collect user information. The app achieves user privacy with the following measures:
  • No user registration: Users do not need to register in order to use the app.
  • No personal information collection beyond GPS location: The app respects data minimization in privacy by design. It does not ask for any sensitive information beyond the optional GPS location. It uses no personal and phone data, such as phone number, contact list, photo gallery, etc.
  • No any upload of user data: Only consolidated CHP publicly available data are downloaded to the users. No any user data leaves the phone to our system. All computations are carried out with results presented locally in one’s phone.
  • No GPS storage at any time: Users may opt to turn on at any time their GPS for live proximity checking and risk assessment. The GPS location, once consumed, is immediately discarded without storage at any time. No personal identifiable information is collected and stored. The app stores only the number of incident places in one’s proximity over a certain past period of time (e.g., 3 days), and the overall proximity risk level. Any data older than the period are purged.
